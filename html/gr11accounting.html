Unit 2: Transactions
T Account -used only for the purpose of explaining account theory
Debit (Dr.)
-left side of an account
-Record amount on the left side of an account
Credit (Cr.)
-right side of an account
-Record amount on the right sides of an account
Debit Accounts -Assets
-Asset account balances are recorded on the debit side
Credit Accounts -Liabilities and Owner’s Equity
- Liabilities and owner’s equity account balances are recorded on the credit side
Recording transaction in T Accounts -an account increases on the same side as its opening balance.
-an account decreases on the opposite side of its opening balance.
Remember -Every account gets a separate T
-Asset balances are recorded on the left side of Asset Accounts
-Liability balances are recorded on the right side of liability accounts
-Owner's Equity balance is recorded on the right side of Owner’s Equity Account
Ledger Group of Accounts
Opening Balance -Amount taken from the balance sheet and entered in an account
-(beginning balance or opening entry).
Account Balance Difference between debit and credit sides of an account
-Assets normally show debit balances
-Liabilities and Owner’s Equity show credit balances
-Enter the Balance on the larger side of the accounts
Trial Balance Procedure for testing the equality of debit and credit account balances
Double Entry Accounting System by which every transaction is recorded with equal debit and credit amounts.
Unit 3: Income Statement
Income Statement -Financial statement which presents the revenue, expenses, and net income or net loss for a specific period of time.
Revenue (income) -inflow of cash/AR from sales of goods and services
-increase in equity as a direct consequence of usual business activity
Expenses -Costs of goods and services used in the operation of a business
-decrease in equity as a direct as a direct consequence of business activity
-Examples
-Salaries
-Advertising
-Hydro
-Insurance
-Internet
Net income (profit) -difference between revenue and expenses when revenue > expenses
Net loss (loss) -difference between revenue and expenses when expenses > revenue
Drawings -Owner;s Personal Withdrawal account
-Withdrawal of assets from the business by the owner
-decrease in equity that does not fit the definition of an expense
Examples of Drawings -withdrawal of cash
-removal of merchandise for personal use
-taking of equipment for personal use
-using company funds for personal expenses.
Accounting Period - fiscal period
- period of time covered by financial statements
Time Period Principle -Requires the definition and use of the same period of time for the accounting period
Matching Principle -costs recorded as expenses should be matched with the revenue they help generate during the same accounting period.
Unit 4: The Journal and the Ledger
The Journal -Journal of business transactions
-Main Journal = General Journal
-”book of original entry”
-transactions recorded chronologically
Journalizing -process of recording transactions in a journal
Journal Entry -Date
-Credit and debit amounts
-explanation of transaction
Steps in Journalizing 1. Record the Date
2. Record the Debit
3. Record the Credit
4. Record the Explanation
Benefits of the Journal -complete transactions in one place
-reduction in errors
-chronological history of ALL transactions
-daily, weekly, monthly value of business activity identified
-snapshot of each day’s transactions
Preparing Accounting Documents
Working papers -Informal documents used to assist in the analysis and organization of information for financial reports
Work Sheet -optional WORKING PAPER that can simplify an accountant’s efforts in preparing financial statements
-not distributed to decision makers
Benefits -preparing in term (monthly or quarterly) financial statements
-linking transactions/events and their effects in financial statements
-avoid errors
-showing effects of proposed or “what if” transactions
What went Wrong
Difference is 1, 10, 100 -Add/subtract error in journal or ledger
Divide the difference by 2 -may be wrong column in trial balance
Difference divisible by 9 -transposition error: 45 written as 54
Difference divisible by 9, difference is by 2 -numbers transposed have a difference of 2: 57 written as 75
Difference overlooked? -Check journal and ledger
Still not found -check ledger
-check pr column
-recheck posting

-up to date record of all transactions affecting a particular account (each account)

The Work Sheet/Classified Statements
Current Assets -Assets expected to be converted within 1 year of the balance sheet date
Fixed Assets -Tangible, long-lived assets held for use within businesses to support revenue-making activities for several years beyond the balance sheet date

Depreciation -Assets wear out and may not retain their original value
-cost principle and matching principle
-often assets contribute to revenues generated over many accounting periods before they wear out.
Capital Assets(fixed assets) -assets purchased for use over long time
-long term-tangible
-intangible assets (patents) that convey the right to use a product or process
-equipment and buildings “wear out” or “decline” in usefulness
-land has an unlimited life; not depreciated
-expenses must be recorded to match the cost of the asset wearing out over the periods benefited
Accumulated Depreciation -total depreciation recorded over the years
original cost - accumulated depreciation = book value
Contra Account -accounts used to offset the value of another account
-has a credit balance
Straight line method -allocate equal amounts of an asset’s net cost over its estimated useful life
-(cost of Asset - Residual value) / estimated useful life = value at end of useful life
Declining Balance Method -allocate asset’s net cost by using a fixed percentage rate
-larger depreciation expense will occur in the early years of an asset’s life with diminishing depreciation expense in late years.
Closing Entries
What is -Journal entries recorded at the end of each accounting period that transfer the end-of-period balances in revenue, expenses and drawings to the permanent owner’s capital account
-update the owner’s capital account for the events of the fiscal period
-prepare for the upcoming period
Temporary Accounts (Nominal Accounts) -Revenue
-Expenses
-Drawings
-Income Summary
Permanent Accounts (real Accounts) -Assets
-Liabilities
-Owner’s Capital
Temporary Accounts -Accumulated Data related to one accounting period
-Describes events or changes that have occurred rather than the financial position that exists at the end of t the period
-Closing process applies only to temporary accounts
Permaenent Accounts -Report on activities related to one or more future accounting periods
-ending balance is carried to next period
-are not close as long the company continues to own the asset, owe the liabilities and have owner’s equity
-describes the existing financial position.
The process -Close revenue
-Close expenses
-Close income summary
-Close drawings
Post-closing Trial Balance -List of permanent accounts and their balance from the ledger after all closing entries are journalised and posted
-list of balances of all accounts not closed.
