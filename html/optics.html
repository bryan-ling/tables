Optics Glossary
light ray -a line of the direction of energy flow (path that light takes)
-the tip of a candle flame emits light in all directions, however we can only see the light that hits our eye.
transparent Allows light to pass through
opaque Does not allow light to pass through
translucent Allows some light to pass through
luminous Generate light energy
non luminous -can only be seen if light hits the object and reflects off of it’s surface
electromagnetic wave -a wave that has both electric and magnetic parts
-does not require a medium
-travels at speed of light
radiation -a method of energy transfer that does not require a medium
-energy travels at speed of light
Incident ray the incoming ray that strikes a surface
Reflection the bouncing back of light from a surface
Law of reflection -The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection
-The incidence ray, reflection ray, and normal all lie in the same plane
Normal perpendicular line to a mirror surface
Plane mirror -plane: flat
-mirror: any polished surface reflecting an image
Image Reproduction of an object through the use of light
Centre of Curvature The centre of the sphere used to make the curve of the mirror
Principal Axis The line through C to the midpoint of the mirror
Focus The point at which light rays parallel to the principal axis converge
Vertex The point at which the principal axis meets the mirror
Converge to meet at a common point
Diverge to separate onto another route
Optical center point at the exact centre of the lens
Converging lens A lens that is thickest in the middle and that causes incident parallel light rays to converge through a single point after refraction.
Diverging lens A lens that is thinnest in the middle and that causes incident parallel light rays to spread apart after refraction
Principle focus the point on the principle axis of a lens where light rays parallel to the principal axis converge after refraction.

Medium any physical substance through which energy can be transferred

-The medium indicates whether it is being illuminated but the object cannot be clearly seen

How is light Produced
Incandescence -light produced as a result of high pressure
-Electricity passes through a filament, causing it to become hot and give off light
Example: Incandescent light bulb
Electric Discharge -Light produced by passing an electric current through gas causing the gas to glow
Example: Neon Sign
Phosphorescence -Phosphors absorb light energy (mostly UV)
-Material keeps some energy and releases rest as light (low energy).
Example: Glow in the dark stickers and toys, watch hands
Fluorescence -Materials absorbs UV light and releases energy as visible light immediately
-Fluorescent light is filled with mercury vapour
-current + mercury = UV light
-UV light absorbed by fluorescent coating on inner surface of tube = visible light
-vs incandescent: 5 times more energy efficient, produce less heat and use less electricity, more expensive but last longer
-need to be properly disposed because they contain mercury
Example: -Fluorescent dyes in detergents make shirts brighter and Highlighters have dyes that glow in presence of UV light
Chemiluminescence -Light produced because of a chemical reaction
-little to no heat is produced
Example: glow sticks
Bioluminescence -Living organisms produce light due to a chemical reaction
-oxygen and luciferin
-used for protection or to attract a mate
Example: firefly
Triboluminescence Light made from friction, by scratching, crushing or rubbing certain crystals
Example: sugar crystals
Light-Emitting Diode(LED) -Light produced as a result of electric current flowing in semiconductors
-Semiconductors allow current to flow in one direction
Example: Christmas lights
Combustion -Reaction involving fuel and oxygen
Example: Fire
Astronomicalํ -Nuclear reaction which is very explosive with lots of energy
Example: Stars
Lasers -produce electromagnetic waves at the same energy level
-waves travel in exactly the same direction
-waves are exactly in unison
-Stands for “Light Amplification by Stimulated Emissions of Radiation”
Mirrors
Why we see things in mirrors -Our eyes are trained to think things are located in the direction the light comes from
-We see things in mirrors because our brain thinks light is coming from behind the mirror
2 rules -distance from the object (dO) is the same as the distance from the image to the mirror (dI), so dO = dI
-The line connecting the object and image intersects the mirror at 90 degrees (perpendicular)
Refraction
Refraction -The bending of light when it moves from one material into another
-Our brain thinks light travels in straight lines so if we put something in water, it isn’t where we think it is.
Less dense to more dense The light ray SLOWS down in the glass, so it bends TOWARDS the normal
More dense to less dense The light ray SPEEDS up in air, so it bends AWAY from the normal
Index of refraction -A number that tells us how the speed of light in space compares with the speed of light in a given substance(medium)
n=c/v
N
-Index of reflection
 C
-speed of light in vacuum
V
-Speed of light in given substance
Another equation for index of refraction -The index of refraction csnl also be calculated using the angle of incidence and the angle of refraction.
n=sin(angle of incidence)/sin(angle of refraction

More Eye Stuff
Accomodation -Eye has ciliary muscles that focus an image onto the retina
-The muscles contract and make lens thinner for far object and relax for near objects
Nearsightedness -Myopia
-Can’t see things unless close
-corrected using diverging lens
-modified is called positive meniscus
Farsightedness -Hyperopia
-Can’t see things unless far
-caused by image focusing behind the retinas
-Eye is too short
-corrected using converging lens
-modified is called negative meniscus
Presbyopia lens that loses elasticity with age and cannot focus near objects onto retina
